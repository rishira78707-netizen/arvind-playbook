"The most common failure mode is not lack of HRBP capability — it is the absence of a structured engagement model. HRBPs cannot deliver strategic impact in an unstructured relationship."

— Gartner Research · Cross-Industry HRBP Effectiveness
The problem this document solves
+
Without structure, HRBPs default to being reactive. Business leaders default to treating HR as a service desk. This document replaces that dynamic with a defined operating model — forums, templates, boundaries, and tools.
An HRBP without this model
+
An HRBP who waits for requests is a service desk. One who only enforces policy is a compliance officer. Neither is a partner. Partnership requires structure — and structure requires this model to be activated, not just read.
What changes when this is in place
+
Business leaders know when to expect HR insight. HRBPs know what to bring and when. Escalation stops feeling like a failure and starts feeling like a protocol. The relationship stops being reactive and starts being structural.
Section 1 · Partnership Model

Not a service arrangement.
A shared accountability.

The HRBP brings workforce data and strategic perspective. The plant head brings business context. Neither makes consistently good people decisions without the other.

What Partnership Means at Arvind
Shared accountability, not a service arrangement.
+
Partnership is a shared accountability for people outcomes — not a service arrangement. The HRBP brings workforce data and strategic perspective; the plant head brings business context. Neither makes consistently good people decisions without the other.

An HRBP who waits for requests is a service desk. One who only enforces policy is a compliance officer. Neither is a partner.
What Business Leaders Must Bring
The partnership works in both directions.
+
The partnership works in both directions. Business leaders must give the HRBP real access: a standing seat in business reviews, honest conversation about people challenges, and the willingness to act on HR insight.

Research from Brandon Hall Group shows CEOs expect HRBPs to interpret talent analytics, enable talent management, and communicate business impact. That expectation is only met when leaders treat the HRBP as a peer.
1.2 · Mutual Commitment Charter

Joint accountability,
documented and visible.

Adapted from Vodafone's HRBP transition model. Joint accountability, documented and visible, was identified as the single most critical ingredient their programme had been missing.

Mutual Commitment Charter
HRBP · Business Leader · Arvind Limited

This charter establishes the terms of the partnership between the HRBP and the Business Leader. Both parties commit to the responsibilities below. The partnership is structural — it holds regardless of who occupies either role. Print, sign, and display at your workstation.

HRBP Commits To
Come to every meeting with data — attrition, bench status, engagement signals — not general impressions
Flag people risks to the plant head before they become crises, not after
Respond to any people-risk escalation within 24 hours
Bring a people agenda item to every business review, even when there is no crisis
Be direct about what is not working, not just what is going well
Propose solutions — not wait to be asked for input
Keep individual employee matters discussed in confidence strictly confidential
Business Leader Commits To
Involve the HRBP in business decisions that have people implications — before the decision is made
Do not route transactional HR queries (payroll, letters, attendance) to the HRBP
Protect the monthly HRBP 1:1 in the calendar. Reschedule only for genuine emergencies
Come to performance calibration sessions with your own assessments — not just sign-offs
Give the HRBP direct feedback on what is useful and what is not
Do not use the HRBP as a messenger, an IR officer, or an administrative resource

1.3 · The Credibility Ladder

Trust is earned
in sequence.

Adapted from Vodafone's HRBP transition model. Each rung is a prerequisite for the next. The most common failure is attempting Rung 4 before building the foundations at Rungs 1 and 2.

What HRBPs often get wrong

Most HRBPs try to jump to Rung 4 — challenging leadership, driving strategy — before they have earned the right to be heard. Rungs 1 and 2 are not optional warm-ups. They are the permission structure for everything that follows.

1
Rung 1
Business Credibility
Read the BU P&L. Understand production targets and cost drivers. Walk the shopfloor. Attend one operations meeting as a listener before contributing.
"The plant head sees you understand their world."
First 30 days
2
Rung 2
Analytical Credibility
Bring attrition data, a succession gap, or an early warning signal to the business leader without being asked. Show them something they did not already know.
"The HRBP is worth listening to."
Days 30–60
3
Rung 3
Execution Credibility
Return to every previous action item with a result, on time. Send a written summary within 24 hours of each meeting.
"The HRBP is reliable."
Month 1 onwards
4
Rung 4
Strategic Credibility
Challenge the business leader on a people risk they are underweighting. Push back on a hiring decision with data. Raise an engagement signal before it becomes a resignation.
"The HRBP is a genuine partner, not a yes-person."
Month 2 onwards
5
Rung 5
Full Partnership
The business leader proactively involves the HRBP in restructuring plans, budget discussions, or leadership appointments — before the decision, not after.
"The HRBP has a standing seat at the table."
Months 3–6
Section 2 · Touchpoint Cadence

Six forums that replace
reactive, ad-hoc HR.

Which of these forums are you running consistently? Most HRBPs have the monthly 1:1 — but skip the quarterly skip-level and the succession review. That gap is exactly where strategic credibility is lost.

5 Non-Negotiable — Cannot be cancelled, shortened, or rescheduled without a genuine emergency
1 Recommended — Target: embedded within 3 months of playbook adoption
1. Monthly HRBP–Business Leader 1:1 Non-Negotiable
Monthly · 45 min HRBP + Business Leader

The HRBP's primary strategic forum. Covers active people risks, succession gaps, talent health, and any decision the business leader needs to make. Cannot be missed or shortened.

2. Quarterly Skip-Level Conversations Non-Negotiable
Quarterly · 60 min HRBP + E-M1 cohort (6–8 employees)

A direct pulse check that bypasses management filters. Captures what is actually happening in the BU before it shows up in exit interviews.

3. Bi-Annual Succession & Talent Review Non-Negotiable
Every 6 months · 90 min HRBP + Business Head + CHRO

The most consequential forum in the model. The HRBP presents bench strength, development plans, and critical role risk ratings. The moment where HRBP credibility is most directly tested.

4. Quarterly Pulse Survey Debrief Non-Negotiable
Quarterly · 45 min HRBP + Business Leader

Translates engagement data into committed action. The HRBP presents a root cause analysis and a three-action plan. Action must be committed before the meeting ends.

5. Quarterly COE Alignment Meeting Recommended
Quarterly · 45 min HRBP + COE Lead (TA / L&D / C&B)

Prevents the most common HRBP model failure: COE designing programmes without BU ground-level input. HRBP brings skill gap data, attrition patterns, and business priorities.

Section 4 · Escalation Pathways

Five scenarios. Triggers,
actions, and hard limits.

Each pathway defines what happens when normal engagement is insufficient. These are not guidelines — they are protocols. Scenario 4 is the one most HRBPs avoid. The rule is direct: decline at the moment of request, not after compliance.

⚑
Trigger
The HRBP has raised the same talent risk in three consecutive monthly 1:1s with no substantive action from the plant head.
→
First Action
HRBP escalates in writing — a brief email to the business leader summarising the three flags, the business consequence if unaddressed, and a specific ask with a deadline.
✓
Resolution
If no response within 5 working days, HRBP informs the HR Head with context — not as a complaint, but as a business risk update that HR leadership needs to be aware of.
✕
Never
Do not raise this in a group setting. Do not involve the CHRO before informing the plant head directly that escalation is coming.
⚑
Trigger
The business leader repeatedly goes directly to Functional Head (IR), the COE, or GCC for M-grade people matters without involving the HRBP.
→
First Action
HRBP raises it directly in the next 1:1: "I have noticed the last three conversations happened without me. I would like to understand what would make it easier to loop me in early."
✓
Resolution
If the pattern continues after the direct conversation, HRBP raises with HR Head. Framing: "The partnership model is not functioning effectively in my BU and I need support to establish it."
✕
Never
Do not raise this as a structural complaint without first attempting a direct conversation with the plant head.
⚑
Trigger
A situation arises requiring a decision beyond the HRBP's authority — a BM resignation risk, a team redesign affecting 15+ roles, a PIP for an M3 employee.
→
First Action
HRBP escalates to the HR Head within 24 hours with: (1) the situation in 3 sentences, (2) the HRBP's recommendation, (3) the timeline pressure, (4) the decision needed and from whom.
✓
Resolution
HRBP does not resolve the situation unilaterally while waiting for escalation response. They manage it to prevent deterioration but do not make the decision.
✕
Never
Do not wait more than 24 hours to escalate a situation that clearly requires authority above your level.
⚑
Trigger
A business leader asks the HRBP to support a decision that conflicts with Arvind's HR policy, employment law, or people values — for example, a forced transfer, a biased shortlist, or a clearly punitive PIP.
→
First Action
HRBP declines clearly and respectfully in the 1:1: "I am not able to support this because [specific reason]. Here is what I can support instead."
✓
Resolution
If the plant head insists, HRBP escalates to the CHRO in writing within 24 hours. This is mandatory, not discretionary. The HRBP's obligation is to the organisation, not to the individual business leader.
✕
Never
Do not comply under pressure and flag it afterwards. Compliance before flagging makes the HRBP complicit.
⚑
Trigger
The HRBP notices a recurring issue that transcends their BU — rising E+ attrition across multiple plants, a manager effectiveness problem appearing in skip-level themes in three different BUs, or a compensation equity issue.
→
First Action
HRBP documents the pattern with data and brings it directly to the CHRO: "I am seeing a pattern across BUs that I think needs enterprise-level attention." This is the HRBP intelligence function at its highest value.
✓
Resolution
CHRO determines whether the response is BU-level (each HRBP acts independently) or requires a coordinated COE-led intervention.
✕
Never
Do not sit on a multi-BU issue because it feels outside your BU scope. Escalating intelligence upward is one of the HRBP's most important functions.
